There's a reason why Rhode Island farmers aren't growing corn and soy on the commodity-crop scale of farmers on the Great Plains; here, the land itself has physical constraints to agricultural development that shape the local food culture and opportunities for profitable farming.

Rhode Island's hilly landscape is dominated by deciduous woodland, punctuated by wetlands, rocky outcroppings, and towns and cities. The farms that do exist are usually fairly small, with very few over 100 acres. This scale and constraints mean that Rhode Island farms are more profitable for growing produce for New England markets, rather than commodity crops. Examining the extent of existing agriculture and the extent of the good soils that are not currently being farmed helps illuminate the potential for agricultural expansion.

Good Soils and Existing Farmland

The first map shows a simple breakdown: land that’s developed (with streets, houses, buildings, infrastructure, etc.) versus land that’s more rural – forested, farmed, or simply unpopulated. Built-up areas are the primary obstacle to the expansion of farming; you can’t farm the middle of a town or airport or power plant!

Good Soils and Existing Farmland

The second map adds more complexity to the story, showing restricted land and existing agriculture in addition to developed land. Conservation land, depicted in olive green, inhibits the expansion of agriculture in rural areas. The northern and central parts of Rhode Island are the least restricted. However, those areas are covered mostly with woodland; is the physical expansion of farmland into wooded areas necessarily a good thing? And when it comes to the large amount of conservation land in the state, should conservation and agriculture be viewed as contradictory in the first place?
