Policy Responses

NYC, NY: New York has been a hotbed for the debate over soda policy for several years. In 2009, Governor David Patterson proposed a 1 cent per ounce soda tax. Proponents of the tax argued that it would bring in billions of dollars in state money, and address the rising obesity rate. Opponents argued that the tax would have a disproportional impact on the poor. The tax was defeated, in large part due to the millions of dollars in anti-tax advertising released by the soda industry. In 2010, Michael Bloomberg, the Mayor of New York City, proposed eliminating soda purchases from the SNAP, or food stamps, program. This proposal was struck down by the USDA, but Bloomberg continued in his efforts to stem soda purchasing. He started pushing for a tax in New York City, and eventually shifted his goal to reducing the size of drink containers used to serve sweetened beverages. According to this ban, sugar-sweetened beverages could not be served in containers larger than 16 ounces. In September 2012, the proposal was approved by the New York City Board of Health. On Monday March 10th, one day before the ban was set to go into effect, a New York State Supreme Court judge struck down the ban. His ruling will likely be appealed.

Richmond, CA: Reuters dubbed the phrase "Soda tax war" when reporting Richmond, California's debates over a ballot measure to tax businesses that sell SSBs, while the New York Times applied the phrase "Big Soda" in a similar article about Richmond. To summarize: advocates of the Richmond tax generally support this kind of policy because they believe the extra cost would deter purchasing and lower consumption, which in turn would also reduce (public) medical costs associated with SSBs, while improving public health overall. Others who oppose SSB taxes often believe that the government should not regulate business purchasing practices and individual beverage preferences, especially because SSBs aren't as harmful to people's health as another taxed substance like tobacco. SSB producers have argued that it is unfair to target soda given that other sugary products like candy or ice cream, for example, are not subject to such regulations. In Richmond, CA, opponents to taxing businesses that sell SSBs (as opposed to individual consumers who buy SSBs) raised the point that even this business-targeted tax would unfairly burden minority-run, small businesses (Peltz, 2013). SSB corporations and lobbying groups spent millions of dollars on advertising to help defeat the tax in Richmond, CA, partnering with groups like the NAACP (Drange, 2012; Confessore, 2013). One public response to this perhaps unlikely partnership was that "Big Soda" was "exploiting race" to defeat the public ballot measure, when Type II Diabetes is more prevalent among African-American and Latino populations (Jonassen, 2013). Most of us involved in this Providence mapping project believe that health disparities have much more to do with all the stressors and hardships that come with poverty, and much less to do with one single, specific product such as soda. Put another way, we think that those from African and Latina/o descent are disproportionately afflicted with these metabolic disorders not because of some ethnic or genetic predisposition, but because they are more likely to be low-income, a unjust reality due to systemic and institutional racism (Glaser, 2005; Marmot, 2005). In short: prejudice causes health disparities, not consumption of SSBs (for recent media and scholarship regarding the role of racism in health outcomes, see Silverstein, 2013). We also wonder how corporations that produce SSBs might be held more accountable for the healthfulness of their products, which are widely marketed and readily available (cite?). What if instead of requiring consumers or businesses to pay a tax, local, state or national governments required SSB manufacturers to meet certain health-related requirements?
