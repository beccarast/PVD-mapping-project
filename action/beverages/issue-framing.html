What others have to say about them...

Lately there's been a lot of discussion in the media and among various public health groups about whether drinking sugar-sweetened beverages (SSBs) causes health problems, particularly Type II Diabetes, and should therefore be taxed, like cigarettes, to help offset the public health costs of treating such diseases (Alliance for a Healther RI, 2012; Caruso & Peltz, 2012; Friedman & Brownell, 2012;Trotta, 2010). Others question the association between soda and health, given that the relationship between soda consumption and diabetes, for example, is not proven to be causal (correlation is not causation) [cite - Guthman?]. This indirect connection differentiates soda from the public health issue of tobacco, because the health effects of drinking soda have not been proven with nearly the same certainty that smoking cigarettes has been shown to cause cancer, among other diseases [Cite]. Further complicating the debate are those who question whether being overweight or obese should even be considered a health "problem," never mind an "epidemic" (Campos et al., 2006). Some argue that focusing on weight as a measure of health is the wrong approach, as weight-focused conversations tend to rely on negative and offensive assumptions about heavier bodies, which in turn contribute to poor self-esteem and additional health problems relating to stress and depression. Equating health with thinness also has the potential to harm thin-bodied people, whose health concerns might be overlooked because they happen to be slim (Bacon & Aphramor, 2011). Additionally, scientists have shown that diet sodas also have negative health effects, an issue that has been largely ignored in the public debate surrounding SSBs (Alphonse, 2013; Fagherazzi et al., 2013). In our research, we sought to broaden this conversation by looking also at corporate ownership of SSBs (inspired by scholar Phil Howard's work), assessing the general availability of SSBs across Providence, and by asking community members their thoughts and opinions on soda consumption, public health and policy.



Our research on soda consumption in Providence has been underway for three years. In its first iteration, students examined the prevalence and variety of sugar-sweetened beverages available within a quarter-mile of Providence public elementary schools. We sought to find out the types and quantities of sodas that young children could access in their walk to and from school. In our second year, we looked at the prevalence of SSB advertising within this same quarter-mile radius. In our third year, students surveyed Providence residents on their opinions about soda policies, especially a proposed soda tax in Rhode Island. These surveys provided the opportunity for follow-up interviews with a number of Providence residents. In our process of interviewing and surveying, we are attempting to gain a better understanding of the public opinion on soda policies.
